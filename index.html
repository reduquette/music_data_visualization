How have songs changed in acousticness, danceability, and energy since 1948?

The blue line represents the acoustic ness of songs throughout the decades. The confidence measure from 0 to 1 of whether the track is acoustic. 1 represents high confidence the track is acoustic. The amount of acousticness in songs had a low dip in 1960 and then went to its max in 1970 before dropping to almost zero.

The green line represents the energy of songs throughout the decades. Energy is measure from 0 to 1 and represents a perceptual measure of intensity and activity. Typically, energetic tracks feel fast, loud, and noisy. For example, death metal has high energy, while a Bach prelude scores low on the scale. Perceptual features contributing to this attribute include dynamic range, perceived loudness, timbre, onset rate, and general entropy. There was a major dip close to zero in 1970 and from there steadily increases with only a small dip in 1990.

The red line represents the danceability of songs throughout the decades. Danceability describes how suitable a track is for dancing based on a combination of musical elements including tempo, rhythm stability, beat strength, and overall regularity. A value of 0 Is the least danceable and 1 is the most danceable. The danceability has remained fairly steady with only a dips in 1970 and a peak in 1990.
